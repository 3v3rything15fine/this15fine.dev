## Reflection

The test development process described across these three readings follows the same general sequence: define the construct, write items, pilot them, analyze the results, revise, and repeat. Price focuses on the statistical tools for evaluating items. The Standards prescribe what must be documented and justified at each stage. Cohen emphasizes qualitative methods and the human context of testing. I found all three useful, but for different reasons.

The Standards' overarching principle (Standard 4.0) is that every design decision should support the validity of score interpretations for their intended uses. This has direct implications for my professional work. The scales we use in our resilience program were developed for populations and purposes that may differ from ours. Whether those instruments produce meaningful scores for our students is an empirical question, not an assumption. These chapters frame that question clearly: the entire test development pipeline, from construct definition through norming, may or may not generalize to a new context. I need to evaluate that rather than take it on faith.

Cohen's treatment of qualitative item analysis was the most immediately useful section for me. Cognitive interviews and think-aloud protocols provide a kind of evidence that item statistics alone cannot. Having students describe their thought process as they respond to survey items would reveal whether they interpret the items as intended, whether the language is clear, and whether cultural references or assumptions create confusion. This is something I can do with a small group of students after administering a scale. It does not require the resources of a large testing program. I had encountered cognitive interviews briefly earlier in the course but did not appreciate how practical they could be until reading Cohen's examples.

Price's treatment of standard setting connected to work I already do. I serve on university committees where we evaluate and set cutoff scores for predictive tools. Price describes standard setting as combining "judgment, psychometric considerations, and the practicality of applying cutoff scores." That matches my experience. In those settings, the psychometric evidence is only as useful as the room's willingness to engage with it. The people at the table do not always share the same incentives, and the technical basis for a given cutoff is not always as transparent as it should be. When I have control over how scores are used, I prefer to examine deviations from the group rather than impose hard cutoffs. A cutoff creates a binary classification from a continuous distribution. The student at 1.49 standard deviations and the student at 1.51 are functionally the same, but a cutoff treats them as categorically different.

## Questions

1. When an instrument is used with a population different from its norming sample, what is the minimum necessary step for establishing that it still produces valid scores? Is running reliability sufficient, or does the evaluation need to include dimensionality and validity evidence as well?

2. Is there a practical framework for conducting cognitive interviews at a small scale? The literature I've encountered on qualitative item analysis is oriented toward large testing programs, but I want to do this with 5 to 10 students after administering a scale in our program.
