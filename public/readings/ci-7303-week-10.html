CI 7303

Norms, Equating, and Survey Quality

Week 10 · Psychometric Methods

Source: Price (2017)

Norms are test performance data collected from a reference group, used to contextualize individual scores. Without norms, a raw score carries no interpretive meaning.

Normative Sample is the specific reference group whose performance data forms the basis for score interpretation. Representativeness of this sample determines the validity of normative comparisons.

Standard Score is a raw score converted to a scale with an arbitrary mean and standard deviation, allowing cross-test comparison.

The 9 steps of a norming study:

  1. Define the target population
  2. Select a sampling strategy (probability sampling preferred over convenience)
  3. Select statistics to compute: mean, SD, variance, skewness, kurtosis, percentile ranks
  4. Determine acceptable sampling error
  5. Acquire the sample and screen data for quality
  6. Compute group-level statistics
  7. Select normative scores (build raw-to-standard conversion tables)
  8. Prepare written documentation
  9. Produce the technical manual

Source: Price (2017)

Raw scores are restrictive. They lack a common metric and cannot be compared across tests or forms. Score transformations solve this problem.

Linear Z-Score Transformation changes only the mean and standard deviation (the first two moments of the distribution). It does not affect skewness or kurtosis. The distribution shape stays the same.

T = AX + B where A = SD_target / SD_raw B = Mean_target - (A x Mean_raw)

Percentile Ranks indicate the percentage of the normative sample scoring at or below a given value.

PR = (cf_lower + 0.5 * f_i) / N x 100

Shortcoming: percentile ranks distort the scale nonlinearly. A one-point difference near the median corresponds to a larger percentile-rank shift than the same difference at the extremes. This makes percentile ranks unsuitable as an equal-interval scale.

Normalized Z-Scores are a nonlinear transformation that forces the distribution to approximate normality. The procedure uses the inverse of the standard normal CDF. Common in IQ and achievement testing where normal distributions are assumed.

Source: Price (2017)

Linking is the broad term for any transformation that maps scores from one test to another. It encompasses a range of procedures varying in strength.

Equating is the strongest form of linking. It produces scores that are truly interchangeable between test forms.

The 5 Rules of Exchangeability (Price, 2017):

  1. Same construct — both forms must measure the same thing
  2. Equal reliability — both forms must have comparable measurement precision
  3. Symmetric function — the equating from X to Y must be the inverse of Y to X
  4. Examinee indifference — it should not matter which form a person takes
  5. Population independence — the equating function should hold across different groups

Horizontal Equating links alternate forms at the same difficulty level.

Vertical Equating links forms across different developmental or grade levels (different difficulty).

Source: Price (2017)

Design I (Random Groups) — Examinees are randomly assigned to either Form X or Form Y. Randomization ensures the groups are equivalent on average. Simple but requires large samples.

Design II (Counterbalanced) — All examinees take both forms in random order. This controls for individual differences but introduces practice and fatigue effects.

Design III (Anchor Test) — Different groups take different forms, but both forms contain a shared set of anchor items (typically about 30% of the test). The anchor items allow statistical adjustment for any group differences.

Design III is the most commonly used in large-scale testing programs because it does not require every examinee to take multiple forms and can adjust for non-equivalent groups.

Source: Price (2017)

Linear Equating matches z-scores between forms. The conversion formula preserves the linear relationship.

Y = (s_y / s_x) * X + [M_y - (s_y / s_x) * M_x]

The Standard Error of Equating (SEE) measures the accuracy of the equating function at each score point.

Equipercentile Equating is nonlinear: it matches scores with identical percentile ranks across forms. Because raw percentile-rank distributions are jagged, smoothing is required.

  • Pre-smoothing (loglinear models) smooths the score distributions before equating
  • Post-smoothing (cubic-spline interpolation) smooths the equating function after computation

IRT Equating bypasses traditional equating by modeling item responses directly on a latent ability scale. Its key advantage is invariance: the ability estimate is independent of the particular items administered and the particular sample tested (Price, 2017).

Anchor test procedure for IRT:

  1. Estimate item difficulty for anchor items in both groups
  2. Compute the mean difference: m = b_group1 - b_group2
  3. Average across all anchor items
  4. Apply the adjustment to place both forms on a common scale

Source: Price (2017), Ch. 5

Price identifies three families of scaling models, distinguished by what they locate on the continuum:

Stimulus-Centered Scaling locates stimuli on a continuum. The goal is to order or space items (e.g., how offensive is each statement on a scale?).

Response-Centered Scaling locates people on a psychological continuum based on their pattern of responses. The items serve as anchors; the person's position is inferred.

Subject-Centered Scaling produces index scores from summed or averaged item responses. This is the most common model in educational measurement. Likert-type instruments are subject-centered scales.

In summary, the three families differ in what is being placed on the scale: the stimuli themselves, the respondents, or a composite index score derived from summed item responses (Price, 2017, Ch. 5).

Source: Price (2017), Ch. 5

Psychophysical Scaling maps stimuli along a physical dimension that is directly measurable (e.g., brightness, weight). The scale can be validated against a known physical quantity.

Psychological Scaling maps people along a latent dimension that has no direct physical referent (e.g., anxiety, self-efficacy). Validity depends on accumulated construct validity evidence, not a physical standard.

This distinction matters because it determines what kind of evidence is needed to evaluate the scale. Price makes the link explicit: scaling is where the abstract construct definition meets the concrete measurement model.

Source: Price (2017), Ch. 5; Stevens (1946)

Stevens (1946) defined four levels: nominal (categories), ordinal (rank order), interval (equal distances, no true zero), and ratio (equal distances, true zero).

The appropriateness of statistical treatment of data depends upon the scales on which measurements are made. — Stevens (1946), as discussed in Price (2017, Ch. 5)

Likert-type scales are ordinal by construction but routinely analyzed with parametric methods that assume interval properties. Is this justified?

Lord and Novick (1968) Position (as discussed in Price, 2017, Ch. 5): Pragmatic. If treating ordinal scores as interval produces more useful information for placement or prediction, treat them as interval. If it does not improve usefulness, use only rank-order information. Whether to treat scores as interval is an empirical question about specific data, not a philosophical commitment.

Price frames this as something that should be evaluated against the distributional properties of the actual data, not resolved by a blanket rule in either direction.

Source: Price (2017), Ch. 5

Thurstone's Equal-Appearing Intervals method uses judges to locate items on a continuum.

  1. Write a large pool of statements about the construct
  2. Judges sort each statement into one of 11 ordered categories
  3. The item's scale value = median category placement across judges
  4. Items with high inter-judge variability are discarded (ambiguous items)
  5. Select items that are evenly spaced across the full range

Key point: judges do not express their own attitudes. They rate where items fall on the continuum, regardless of personal agreement. The respondent then endorses items, and their score is the median scale value of endorsed items.

Source: Price (2017), Ch. 5

Likert Scaling is a summated rating approach. Each item has 5 to 7 response options (e.g., Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree). The total score is the sum or average of item responses.

Item selection criterion: item-total correlation. Items that discriminate well between high and low total-score groups are retained. Items with low item-total correlations are dropped because they do not contribute to measuring the construct.

Likert scaling is the most common format in educational and psychological research. The instruments Jason uses in B3 are Likert-type summated rating scales.

Source: Price (2017), Ch. 5

Guttman Scaling (scalogram analysis) assumes items can be ordered by difficulty or intensity such that endorsing a harder item implies endorsing all easier items. This cumulative property is the defining feature.

Example: a student who reports attending office hours should also report attending class. If the pattern breaks, either the construct is not unidimensional or the items are not measuring what was intended.

Coefficient of Reproducibility measures how well actual response patterns match the ideal cumulative pattern. Values above .90 suggest acceptable scalability, though the model rarely holds perfectly in practice.

The Guttman model is most useful as a design principle: if you are building a scale to measure progressive levels (e.g., engagement intensity), items should exhibit something like a cumulative structure.
