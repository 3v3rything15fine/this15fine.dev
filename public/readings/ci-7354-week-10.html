Five Qualitative Approaches

Creswell Ch. 4 — Narrative, Phenomenology, Grounded Theory, Ethnography, Case Study

0/8 sections 0/25 cards quiz: --

Shared Process

All five approaches follow a common sequence:

  1. Research Problem
  2. Research Questions
  3. Data Collection
  4. Data Analysis
  5. Research Report

Selection Criteria

The approach you choose depends on what you want to study:

  • Narrative — stories of individual experience
  • Phenomenology — essence of a lived experience
  • Grounded Theory — theory grounded in participant data
  • Ethnography — cultural patterns of a group
  • Case Study — in-depth analysis of a bounded system
Exploring the life of an individual through chronologically connected stories.

Origins

Humanities, anthropology, psychology, sociology.

Key Features

  • Collect stories collaboratively with participants
  • Focus on individual experiences
  • Identify epiphanies and turning points
  • Shape data into chronology (restorying)

Types

Biographical Study Autoethnography Life History Oral History

7 Procedures

  1. Determine if narrative is suitable for the research problem
  2. Select one or more participants who have stories to tell
  3. Consider how data will be shaped (field texts, conversations, journals)
  4. Collect stories within the participant's context
  5. Analyze and restory into a chronological framework
  6. Collaborate with participants; negotiate meaning
  7. Present the narrative in the research report

Challenges

  • Understanding the participant's complex life context
  • The "figure under the carpet" — hidden meanings below the surface
  • Ownership: who owns the story?
Describes the common meaning of a lived experience for several individuals, reducing it to a central phenomenon (the "essence").

Philosophical Roots

  • Search for wisdom
  • Intentionality of consciousness (experience is always directed toward something)
  • Suspend judgments (bracketing / epoché)
  • Refuse the subject-object dichotomy

Key Features

  • Single concept or phenomenon (e.g., "grief")
  • Heterogeneous group sharing the experience
  • Researcher brackets their own experience
  • Typically interviews as primary data

Types

Hermeneutical (van Manen) — interpreting the "texts" of life; meaning through interpretation.

Transcendental (Moustakas) — epoché/bracketing, textural + structural descriptions leading to essence.

Procedures

  1. Identify the phenomenon to study
  2. Recognize and state philosophical assumptions
  3. Collect data from those who have experienced the phenomenon
  4. Generate significant statements and meaning units
  5. Develop textural descriptions (what) and structural descriptions (how)
  6. Report the composite essence of the experience

Challenges

  • Genuinely bracketing personal experience
  • Defining and maintaining epoché
Systematic generation of a theory — a "unified theoretical explanation" — grounded in participant data rather than pre-existing frameworks.

Origins

Sociology (Glaser & Strauss, 1967).

Key Features

  • Study a process or action with distinct steps over time
  • Simultaneous, iterative data collection and analysis
  • Constant comparative method and memo writing
  • Typically 20–60 interviews

Types

Systematic (Strauss & Corbin) — structured coding sequence, conditional matrix, theoretical sampling.

Constructivist (Charmaz) — flexible, acknowledges multiple realities, active codes using gerunds (e.g., "negotiating," "adapting").

Coding Phases

  1. Open coding — identify main categories from the data
  2. Axial coding — relate categories around a core phenomenon: causal conditions, strategies, intervening conditions, consequences
  3. Selective coding — develop propositions/hypotheses leading to a substantive theory

Challenges

  • Setting aside pre-existing theoretical frameworks
  • Determining when category saturation has been reached
Describing and interpreting the values, behaviors, beliefs, and language of a culture-sharing group.

Origins

Anthropology, sociology.

Key Features

  • Immersion in the cultural group
  • Extended fieldwork over a prolonged period
  • Participant observation as primary method
  • Emic (insider) perspective alongside etic (outsider) interpretation
  • Cultural interpretation — making meaning from patterns

Understanding Culture

Culture is inferred from words and actions. It encompasses behaviors, language, and the tension between what people say is and what they believe ought to be.

Types

Realist — objective, third-person account using standard categories of cultural description.

Critical — focused on emancipation of marginalized groups; addresses power, control, and social inequity.

Procedures

  1. Determine if ethnography is appropriate for the problem
  2. Locate a culture-sharing group and gatekeeper
  3. Identify cultural themes to explore
  4. Determine the type of ethnography (realist, critical, etc.)
  5. Conduct extended fieldwork: observation, interviews, artifacts
  6. Develop a cultural portrait of the group
  7. Present findings with cultural interpretation

Challenges

  • Extensive time commitment for fieldwork
  • "Going native" — losing objectivity by over-identifying with the group
  • Gaining access and managing gatekeeper relationships
  • Cultural sensitivity and ethical representation
In-depth analysis of one or more bounded systems in a real-life contemporary context, using multiple sources of information.

Origins

Psychology, law, political science, medicine.

Key Features

  • Multiple sources of information (interviews, documents, observation, artifacts)
  • Bounded system — defined by time, place, or context
  • Can be multisite or within-site

Three Intents

Single Instrumental — one issue explored through one case to illuminate a broader concern.

Collective / Multiple — one issue explored across multiple cases (limit to 4–5).

Intrinsic — the unusual case itself is the focus, not a broader issue.

Procedures

  1. Determine if case study fits the research problem
  2. Identify case(s) using purposeful sampling
  3. Collect extensive data from multiple sources
  4. Analyze holistically, in embedded units, or thematically
  5. Develop assertions, generalizations, or lessons learned

Challenges

  • Clearly bounding the case
  • Providing rationale for sampling decisions
  • For multiple case studies: limiting to 4–5 cases for depth

Unit of Analysis

  • Narrative — 1+ individuals
  • Phenomenology — a shared lived experience
  • Grounded Theory — a process or action
  • Ethnography — a culture-sharing group
  • Case Study — a bounded system

Data Collection

Interviews dominate narrative, phenomenology, and grounded theory. Ethnography adds sustained observation. Case study demands the most diverse sources.

Final Product

  • Narrative — restoried chronological account
  • Phenomenology — description of essence
  • Grounded Theory — theoretical model
  • Ethnography — cultural portrait
  • Case Study — in-depth descriptions + assertions

Creswell's Framework

  1. Identify the research topic
  2. Determine if qualitative, quantitative, or mixed methods is appropriate
  3. Anticipate at least three ethical issues and develop strategies to address each

Each approach carries its own ethical weight. Narrative research raises questions of story ownership. Phenomenology requires honest bracketing. Ethnography demands cultural sensitivity and power awareness. All approaches require informed consent and protection of participant identity.
