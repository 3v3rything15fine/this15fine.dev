# Reading Reflection 4: Test Development -- Chapter Summaries

**Chapters:** Price (2017) Ch. 6, Standards (AERA, 2014) Ch. 4, Cohen et al. (2021) Ch. 8

---

## 1. Price (2017) -- Chapter 6: Test Development

### Key Concepts

1. **The 11-Guideline Framework for Test Development.** Price organizes the entire test development process into a sequential set of 11 guidelines: articulate a philosophical foundation, identify the test purpose, select attributes reflective of the construct, identify the examinee population, delineate item content, write items, develop administration procedures, conduct pilot testing, conduct item and factor analyses, develop norms, and write the technical manual. This framework provides a coherent, step-by-step roadmap that makes the process feel manageable rather than overwhelming.

2. **The Test Blueprint (Table of Specifications).** A two-way grid specifying content areas, number of items, content weight, and cognitive skill levels (e.g., comprehension vs. application from Bloom's taxonomy). The blueprint forces the developer to make explicit decisions about emphasis and alignment before writing a single item. Price demonstrates this concretely through the GfGc intelligence theory example.

3. **Item Difficulty (Proportion Correct) Is Not as Simple as It Sounds.** Item difficulty is the proportion of examinees responding correctly, but the label is counterintuitive: a *higher* p-value means an *easier* item. Price emphasizes that difficulty interacts with discrimination and must be considered relative to the test's purpose, not in isolation. He also introduces the optimal difficulty formula that accounts for guessing (Equation 6.1).

4. **Item Discrimination and the D-Index.** Discrimination measures how well an item separates high-ability from low-ability examinees. Price provides Ebel and Frisbie's screening criteria (.40+ = very good, below .19 = poor) and makes the critical point that an item with optimal difficulty does not automatically have good discrimination -- the two must be evaluated together.

5. **Point-Biserial and Biserial Correlations.** These correlation-based indices of item discrimination are used more frequently than the D-index in practice. The point-biserial estimates the relationship between a dichotomous item score (0/1) and the total test score. Price notes that for tests with fewer than 25 items, the item under study should be removed from the total score calculation to avoid spurious inflation.

6. **Item Reliability Index and Item Validity Index -- Used Together.** The item reliability index (standard deviation of the item multiplied by the item-total correlation) indicates internal consistency at the item level. The item validity index (standard deviation multiplied by the item-criterion correlation) indicates criterion-related validity. Price recommends plotting these together (Figure 6.5) and selecting items farthest from the upper-left corner to achieve the best balance of both.

7. **Standard Setting and the Borderline Examinee.** Price devotes substantial attention to cutoff-score methods (Nedelsky, Ebel, Angoff, Bookmark), all of which rely on subject-matter experts making judgments about what a "borderline examinee" can do. The Atkins v. Virginia example -- where an IQ score determined whether someone stayed on death row -- makes the stakes of classification decisions viscerally clear.

8. **Subject-Matter Experts Are Central Throughout.** Experts contribute at nearly every stage: identifying construct attributes, conducting content analysis, evaluating items, and setting cutoff scores. The process is described as iterative and cyclical -- "until no new information is garnered" -- which underscores that test development is collaborative and not purely statistical.

### Unique Contributions

- Price provides the most **technically detailed** treatment of item analysis statistics, including worked examples with SPSS syntax, BILOG-MG output, and full item-level data tables for a 25-item crystallized intelligence subtest. Neither the Standards nor Cohen provides this level of computational specificity.
- The **four standard-setting methods** (Nedelsky, Ebel, Angoff, Bookmark) are treated at greater length here than in either of the other readings. Price explains each method's mechanics, strengths, and limitations.
- The **tetrachoric correlation** is discussed as the preferred coefficient for factor-analyzing dichotomously scored items from a normally distributed construct -- a subtle technical point the other chapters do not address.
- Price provides multiple classification frameworks (Bloom's taxonomy, Ebel's relevance guide, Gagne's learning outcomes) for categorizing items in a test blueprint, offering concrete guidance for specification decisions.

### Practical Connections

- **Test blueprints and specification tables** are directly relevant to anyone who administers surveys or scales. Even when using an existing instrument (like a resilience or engagement scale), understanding the blueprint behind it helps evaluate whether the instrument adequately covers the construct as intended for your population.
- **The item reliability vs. item validity tradeoff** matters when selecting or modifying scales for use in a student success program. An item that contributes strongly to internal consistency may not predict the outcome you care about (e.g., retention), and vice versa.
- **Standard-setting methods** apply whenever cutoff scores are used to classify students -- for example, flagging students as "at risk" based on an engagement score. Price's discussion makes clear that these decisions blend expert judgment with psychometric evidence and should not be treated as purely objective.
- **The emphasis on sampling** (Guidelines 4 and 8) is a reminder that pilot testing with a representative sample matters. A resilience scale developed and normed on a different population may not function the same way with your students.

### Potential Discussion Points

- **The counterintuitive naming of "item difficulty."** A higher p-value = an easier item. This is one of those things that trips up newcomers every time, and Price does not dwell on why it is confusing -- it might be worth reflecting on how naming conventions in psychometrics can obscure rather than clarify.
- **The gap between theory and practice in standard setting.** All four methods rely heavily on expert judgment about a "borderline examinee," which is hypothetical. Students might find it surprising how much subjective judgment goes into what appears to be an objective cutoff score.
- **Most valuable for a student at this level:** The 11-guideline framework as a whole -- it provides a mental model for how all the pieces (validity, reliability, item analysis, norms) fit together in practice, rather than existing as isolated concepts.

### Notable Quotes or Passages

1. > "Defining the content to be sampled by the test or instrument is likely the most important core exercise in test and/or instrument development if any valid score-based inferences are to be made. No other component of test or instrument construction is as important as identifying and articulating the domain of content to be sampled."

2. > "A test item displaying optimal difficulty does not ensure a high level of discrimination."

3. > "The practice of standard setting combines judgment, psychometric considerations, and the practicality of applying cutoff scores."

4. > "Because placement and selection are activities that have a high impact on people's lives, careful consideration is crucial to prevent misclassification. For example, consider a child who is incorrectly classified as being learning disabled based on his or her test score."

5. > "The term high quality means that the data are objective, defensible, and reproducible."

## 2. Standards for Educational and Psychological Testing (AERA, 2014) -- Chapter 4: Test Design and Development

### Key Concepts

1. **Test Development Must Be Driven by Intended Interpretations and Uses.** The Standards frame every design decision -- content, format, length, scoring, administration -- as flowing from the statement of purpose and intended score interpretations. This is not just a recommendation; it is the overarching standard (4.0). The emphasis is on supporting validity of intended interpretations, not just building a "good test" in the abstract.

2. **Test Specifications Are Broadly Defined.** Unlike colloquial usage where "test specifications" might mean just a content outline, the Standards define specifications to include purpose, intended uses, content, format, test length, psychometric properties, delivery mode, administration procedures, scoring, and reporting. This is a comprehensive design document, not a simple blueprint.

3. **Fairness and Construct-Irrelevant Variance Are Design-Level Concerns.** The Standards emphasize that fairness is not an afterthought addressed after the test is built. Format specifications should consider accessibility and universal design. Items should be screened for sensitivity, offensiveness, and differential item functioning (DIF) during development. If test questions require linguistic skills not relevant to the construct, they introduce construct-irrelevant variance that undermines validity and fairness.

4. **The Iterative Nature of Test Development.** The Standards describe a four-phase process: (a) developing test specifications, (b) developing and trying out items, (c) assembling and evaluating test forms, and (d) developing administration and scoring procedures. Importantly, this process is described as "commonly iterative, with adjustments and revisions made in response to data from tryouts and operational use." Specifications themselves may need refinement as development proceeds.

5. **Complex Item Formats (Performance Assessments, Simulations, Portfolios).** The Standards provide detailed guidance on these formats, noting that because performance assessments typically involve a small number of tasks, establishing generalizability to the broader domain is "particularly important." Scoring rubrics -- analytic vs. holistic -- must reflect the test framework and be developed alongside the items.

6. **Scoring and Scorer Quality Are Part of Test Development.** Standards 4.18-4.21 address scoring with specificity: rubrics must be detailed, scorers must be trained and monitored, anchor protocols should be used, and scoring consistency must be documented. When automated scoring algorithms are used, their theoretical and empirical bases must be documented and checked for systematic bias.

7. **Test Revision Is an Ongoing Obligation.** Standard 4.24 states that tests should be amended or revised when new research, domain changes, or new conditions of use reduce validity. Standard 4.25 requires informing users of specification changes and score comparability when tests are revised. This positions test development as a lifecycle, not a one-time event.

8. **Documentation Is Not Optional.** Nearly every standard includes a documentation requirement. The rationale, the process, the qualifications of reviewers, the results of reviews, the psychometric model used, the DIF procedures -- all must be documented. This is the Standards' way of ensuring transparency and accountability.

### Unique Contributions

- The Standards is the only chapter written as an **authoritative, consensus-based professional standard**. It does not teach methods; it prescribes what must be done and documented. This gives it a different rhetorical force than the textbook chapters.
- **Adaptive testing and computerized test delivery** receive more detailed treatment here than in Price or Cohen, including item selection algorithms, item pool size, content coverage constraints, context effects, and security concerns.
- **The emphasis on fairness throughout the development process** is more sustained and structural here than in the other readings. DIF analysis, sensitivity reviews, universal design, and accommodations are woven into specifications rather than treated as separate topics.
- **External expert review** (Standard 4.6) is explicitly called for when the stakes are high, including documentation of reviewers' qualifications and demographic characteristics. This level of process accountability is unique to the Standards.
- **The distinction between data-driven and theory-driven test development** is made explicit: empirically keyed tests (where items are selected based on criterion correlations) require cross-validation to guard against chance selection.

### Practical Connections

- **Standard 4.0's principle** -- that tests should be designed to support the validity of score interpretations for their intended uses -- is directly relevant when selecting instruments for a student success program. The question is not "Is this a good scale?" but "Does this scale support the specific interpretations I need to make with my specific population?"
- **Construct-irrelevant variance** is a practical concern for anyone administering surveys to diverse undergraduate populations. If a resilience scale uses vocabulary or cultural references unfamiliar to some students, it introduces irrelevant variance that undermines the scores' meaning for those students.
- **Scoring standards** apply even to locally administered scales. If multiple staff members score open-ended responses or make judgments based on scale results, the Standards' guidance on training, monitoring, and documenting consistency is relevant.
- **Test revision standards** are a reminder that instruments used in ongoing programs should be reviewed periodically. Norms may become outdated, populations shift, and the construct itself may be reconceptualized.

### Potential Discussion Points

- **The documentation burden.** The Standards require extensive documentation at every stage. A student might wonder how realistic this is outside of large testing programs -- and that tension is worth exploring. Even in small-scale contexts, the spirit of these standards (be explicit about your decisions and their rationale) is valuable.
- **The interplay between fairness and validity.** The Standards treat these as deeply intertwined rather than separate concerns. This may be counterintuitive for students accustomed to thinking of fairness as an ethical overlay on top of technical quality.
- **Most valuable for a student at this level:** The idea that test development is iterative and that specifications themselves evolve. This challenges the implicit assumption that a test is "finished" when published.

### Notable Quotes or Passages

1. > "Tests and testing programs should be designed and developed in a way that supports the validity of interpretations of the test scores for their intended uses." (Standard 4.0)

2. > "If test questions require test takers to possess significant linguistic skill to interpret them but the test is not intended as a measure of linguistic skill, the complexity of the questions may lead to construct-irrelevant variance in test scores."

3. > "Test design and development procedures must support the validity of the interpretations of test scores for their intended uses."

4. > "Test specifications should be amended or revised when new research data, significant changes in the domain represented, or newly recommended conditions of test use may reduce the validity of test score interpretations." (Standard 4.24)

5. > "The increased reliance on technology and the need for speed and efficiency in the test development process require consideration of the systems supporting item and test development."

## 3. Cohen, Schneider, & Tobin (2021) -- Chapter 8: Test Development

### Key Concepts

1. **The Five-Stage Model: Conceptualization, Construction, Tryout, Item Analysis, Revision.** Cohen organizes test development into five stages and emphasizes that the cycle is iterative -- tryout and item analysis may send the developer back to revision multiple times. The chapter opens with the clear framing that "the creation of a good test is not a matter of chance. It is the product of the thoughtful and sound application of established principles of test development."

2. **Test Conceptualization as Asking Hard Questions.** Before any items are written, Cohen poses a series of pointed questions the developer must answer: What is the test designed to measure? What is the objective? Is there a need for this test? Who will use it? Who will take it? What content will it cover? These questions force clarity about purpose before committing to format or items.

3. **Scaling Methods Shape Measurement.** Cohen provides a thorough survey of scaling methods: Likert scales, Thurstone's equal-appearing intervals, Guttman scales, paired comparisons, comparative and categorical scaling, summative scales. The key insight is that there is no one "best" scale type -- the choice depends on what is being measured, who is being measured, and the developer's conceptual framework.

4. **Norm-Referenced vs. Criterion-Referenced: Different Development Logics.** Cohen distinguishes these clearly: a good norm-referenced item discriminates between high and low scorers; a good criterion-referenced item assesses whether the test-taker has met defined criteria regardless of relative standing. This distinction drives fundamentally different approaches to item analysis and selection.

5. **Item-Characteristic Curves (ICCs) as Visual Tools.** Cohen uses ICCs to make item quality visually intuitive: a steep slope means good discrimination, leftward shift means easy, rightward means hard. The examples of "bad" ICCs (Item A where low-ability test-takers outperform high-ability ones, Item B where moderate-ability peaks) are pedagogically effective for understanding what makes an item work or fail.

6. **Qualitative Item Analysis Deserves Equal Attention.** Cohen dedicates significant space to qualitative methods: think-aloud protocols, expert panels, sensitivity reviews, and post-test interviews with test-takers. The table of potential qualitative exploration topics (cultural sensitivity, face validity, test fairness, test-taker mental/physical state) provides a practical template. This is a notable counterweight to the chapters' otherwise quantitative emphasis.

7. **Cross-Validation and Validity Shrinkage.** Cohen explains that item validities will inevitably shrink when the test is administered to a new sample, and this is expected and healthy. Using the same sample for development and validation produces "spuriously high" validity estimates. This concept of validity shrinkage is a critical check on overconfidence in initial results.

8. **IRT vs. CTT: Trade-offs in Test Development.** The CTT-vs.-IRT comparison table is a concise, practical summary. Key points: CTT item statistics are sample-dependent (a major limitation); IRT provides sample-independent item parameters but requires larger samples and stronger assumptions. Cohen notes that IRT enables adaptive testing and more efficient measurement.

### Unique Contributions

- **The pedagogical, conversational tone** is unique to Cohen. "Just Think..." prompts throughout the chapter invite the reader to apply concepts actively. The real-world case studies (AIS asexuality scale, HANAA Aboriginal assessment, Dr. Birkeland's industrial testing) make test development feel like a human activity, not just a technical one.
- **Qualitative item analysis** receives far more treatment here than in Price or the Standards. The think-aloud protocol method, expert sensitivity panels, and the detailed table of qualitative questions to ask test-takers are practical tools not found in the other readings.
- **The Asexuality Identification Scale (AIS) case study** provides a start-to-finish example of developing a new instrument for an underserved population, including known-groups validity, incremental validity, convergent validity, and discriminant validity -- all within a single, coherent narrative.
- **The "yarning" approach to Aboriginal assessment** illustrates how cultural considerations fundamentally reshape test design, not just translation. This is a powerful example of how construct-irrelevant variance operates in cross-cultural assessment.
- **Co-validation and co-norming** -- the practice of validating multiple tests on the same sample -- is discussed only in Cohen. This is an increasingly common practice (e.g., Wechsler scales) that eliminates sampling error as a rival explanation for score differences across co-normed instruments.
- **Scoring models (cumulative, class/category, ipsative)** are explained with clarity. The ipsative scoring example (EPPS) includes the crucial caveat that ipsative scores support only within-person comparisons, not between-person comparisons -- a restriction that is easy to violate in practice.
- **The CTT vs. IRT comparison table** provides a side-by-side summary not found in the other readings, making the practical trade-offs accessible to someone still learning.

### Practical Connections

- **The conceptualization questions** are directly useful for anyone evaluating an existing instrument: Does this scale measure what I need? Is it designed for my population? What would scores mean in my context? Going through Cohen's list of questions with an existing instrument is a practical evaluation exercise.
- **Qualitative item analysis methods** are applicable in program evaluation contexts. After administering a new survey to students, conducting think-aloud sessions or post-survey interviews with a subset of respondents can reveal whether items are interpreted as intended -- especially important for diverse student populations.
- **The norm-referenced vs. criterion-referenced distinction** matters for how student success programs use scale scores. If you are using an engagement scale to classify students as "at risk" (criterion-referenced logic), you need items selected for that purpose, not items optimized for spreading out a distribution (norm-referenced logic).
- **Floor and ceiling effects** are practical concerns when scales developed for general populations are administered to specific subpopulations. A resilience scale normed on broad college samples may have a ceiling effect with high-functioning students, failing to distinguish among them.
- **Sensitivity reviews and fairness** are especially relevant for instruments used with diverse undergraduate populations. Cohen's list of possible content biases (status, stereotype, familiarity, offensive language) provides a practical checklist.

### Potential Discussion Points

- **The problem of guessing has no universally accepted solution.** Cohen is refreshingly honest about this: "To date, no solution to the problem of guessing has been deemed entirely satisfactory." Students might find it surprising that such a basic measurement problem remains unsolved.
- **The tension between speed and accuracy on timed tests** produces misleading item statistics. Items near the end of a speed test appear artificially difficult and artificially discriminating. This is counterintuitive and important to understand.
- **Ipsative scoring feels natural but limits interpretation.** The idea that you cannot compare two people's scores on an ipsatively scored test -- even though the scores look like they should be comparable -- is a common source of misinterpretation.
- **Most valuable for a student at this level:** The emphasis on qualitative item analysis. Most psychometrics courses lean heavily quantitative; Cohen's argument that talking to test-takers is a legitimate and important form of item analysis expands the student's methodological toolkit.

### Notable Quotes or Passages

1. > "All tests are not created equal. The creation of a good test is not a matter of chance. It is the product of the thoughtful and sound application of established principles of test development."

2. > "If items for a personality inventory are being administered in an atmosphere that encourages frankness and the eventual test is to be administered in an atmosphere where subjects will be reluctant to say bad things about themselves, the item analysis will tell a faulty story." (citing Nunnally, 1978)

3. > "Any persons showing the same ability as measured by the whole test should have the same probability of passing any given item that measures that ability, regardless of the person's race, social class, sex, or any other background characteristics." (citing Jensen, 1980, on the rationale for DIF analysis)

4. > "The decrease in item validities that inevitably occurs after cross-validation of findings is referred to as validity shrinkage. Such shrinkage is expected and is viewed as integral to the test development process."

5. > "Any improvement or decrement in performance between the two [test versions] cannot automatically be viewed as a change in examinee performance." (citing Tulsky & Ledbetter, 2000, on comparing original and revised editions)

## Cross-Chapter Synthesis

### Where All Three Overlap

- All three chapters agree that test development begins with a clear statement of purpose and construct definition, and that this foundational step shapes every subsequent decision.
- All three emphasize that item analysis (difficulty, discrimination, reliability, validity) is essential for selecting good items and that pilot testing with representative samples is a non-negotiable step.
- All three treat fairness and bias as concerns that must be addressed during development, not after.
- All three describe test development as iterative: items are tried, analyzed, revised, and re-tried.

### Where They Diverge

| Dimension | Price (2017) | Standards (2014) | Cohen et al. (2021) |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Tone** | Technical textbook | Authoritative professional standard | Pedagogical, conversational |
| **Primary audience** | Psychometrics students and researchers | Test developers and publishers | Introductory-level students |
| **Depth of item analysis** | Very deep (formulas, SPSS output, worked examples) | Prescriptive but not computational | Moderate (formulas explained conceptually) |
| **Standard setting** | Extensive (4 methods detailed) | Referenced but not elaborated | Not covered |
| **Qualitative methods** | Minimal | Mentioned (cognitive labs) | Substantial (think-aloud, expert panels, interviews) |
| **Fairness/DIF** | Brief mention | Structural, woven throughout | Case-study-driven (AIS, Aboriginal assessment) |
| **Adaptive testing** | Not covered | Detailed | Covered (CAT, item branching) |
| **IRT** | Referenced, deferred to Ch. 7 | Referenced as a tool | CTT vs. IRT comparison table |
| **Real-world examples** | Intelligence testing (GfGc) | General across testing programs | Clinical, educational, industrial, cultural |

### For the Reflection Paper

The most productive angle for a reflection paper is probably the tension between the *precision* these chapters demand and the *messiness* of real-world assessment contexts. Price provides the statistical machinery for evaluating items; the Standards insist that every decision be documented and justified; Cohen reminds us that talking to actual test-takers matters and that cultural context shapes everything. A student working in higher education advising -- administering scales that were likely developed elsewhere, for different populations -- sits at the intersection of all three perspectives. The reflection could explore: What happens when a well-developed instrument meets a context it was not designed for? What does "good enough" look like when full-scale test development is not feasible, but the principles still apply?
